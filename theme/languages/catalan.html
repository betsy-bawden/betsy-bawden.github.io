About

Catalan is the second most spoken language in Spain (after Spanish). It is spoken by roughly 17% of the population as either a first or second language. The majority of speakers live in the region of Catalonia, although it is also spoken in other regions such as Valencia.

History

The Catalan language evolved from Vulgar Latin during the middle ages. It was spoken throughout the western half of Spain, as well as in parts of Italy and France. However, as Spanish became more standardized around the 15th century, the use of Catalan began to decrease.

In 1789, shortly after the French Revolution, the use of Catalan was prohibited in the south of France, where there was a significant number of speakers.

The use of the language continued to decline in Spain and the surrounding countries until the 19th century, when Catalan saw a literary revival. However, the Franco dictatorship banned the use of Catalan in schools and public administration between 1939 and 1975.

Present Day

Since the Spanish transition towards democracy, Catalan has regained its official status. The majority of the population of Catalonia is bilingual. Approximately 9 million people around the world can speak it, while 11 million can understand it. The majority of the speakers live in Spain, although there are minority groups in both Italy and France that have a significant number of speakers. Additionally, Catalan is the official language of Andorra, a small country between Spain and France where there are around 70,000 speakers.

Within Spain, the language currently has official status in Catalonia, Valencia, the Balearic Islands, and La Franja (a small community in Aragonia).
